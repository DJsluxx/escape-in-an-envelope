Halloween Party Games for Kids (Spooky, Not Scary)

A brilliant kids' Halloween party doesn't need a haunted-house budget or a single genuine scare — it needs a handful of friendly-spooky games, a plan so you're not improvising when a room full of little monsters arrives at once, and enough candy-fuelled fun to wear them out. Here's a complete, do-it-yourself Halloween party plan you can actually pull off: a timeline that flows, games kids love, easy spooky food, cheap decorations, favours worth keeping, and one shopping list to grab it all.

🎃 A kids' Halloween party timeline that actually flows

The secret to a party that stays fun and doesn't tip into overtired-and-sugar-crashed chaos is a loose plan you can flex. Aim for about two hours for ages 6-9 — long enough to run several games, short enough to wrap before the candy wears off. Open with a settling activity while stragglers arrive, keep the big-energy games early, and save the shared mission for the end when they're primed for a story.

Here's a running order you can copy and adjust to your little coven:

  1. 0:00–0:15 — Arrival: a costume parade and a colouring or mask-decorating table while everyone turns up
  2. 0:15–0:50 — Active games while they're fresh: pumpkin bowling, mummy-wrap race, doughnuts-on-a-string
  3. 0:50–1:10 — Spooky feast and cake
  4. 1:10–1:40 — The main event: one shared spooky mission across the 'haunted' house
  5. 1:40–2:00 — Prizes, treat bags, and a calm wind-down before pick-up

Halloween party games kids actually love

You only need four or five games for a two-hour party — resist the urge to over-plan. Mix a couple of high-energy runarounds with one or two calmer games so little ones can catch their breath between the screams of laughter. These are the ones that reliably land with 6-9 year olds and stay firmly on the friendly side of spooky:

  • Pumpkin bowling — line up six toilet rolls (or empty bottles) drawn with monster faces and roll a small pumpkin or orange to knock them down. Endless resets, zero cost, and toddlers can play too.
  • Mummy-wrap race — split into pairs and race to wrap one child head-to-toe in toilet paper. Fastest, neatest mummy wins. Chaotic, hilarious, and the photos are gold.
  • Doughnuts on a string — hang ring doughnuts from a broom handle or washing line and let kids eat them with no hands. Messy, funny, and doubles as a snack.
  • Monster feely-box — a cardboard box with a hand-hole and bowls of 'monster guts': cold spaghetti (worms), peeled grapes (eyeballs), a peeled tomato (a heart). Squeals guaranteed, and gentle enough to skip for nervous guests.
  • Musical monsters — freeze-dance to a playful spooky playlist; when the music stops everyone strikes a monster pose and the wobbliest ghoul is out (or, for little ones, nobody's out and everyone just poses).

Spooky food that's easy (and barely any work)

Halloween food is all about the name and a googly eye, not the labour. Rename ordinary snacks with spooky names, stick a pair of edible eyes on things, and a plain platter becomes a monster feast. You do not need to bake anything ambitious.

One shortcut that saves your sanity: print little tombstone-shaped tent cards and prop one in front of each dish — the naming does all the theming for you, and it turns a normal party spread into 'the feast'.

And one safety note that takes thirty seconds — for younger guests, halve grapes and cherry tomatoes lengthways and skip cocktail sticks. Then you can relax and enjoy the party too.

  • 'Witches' fingers' — pretzel rods or cheese straws with a flaked-almond 'nail'
  • 'Monster eyeballs' — mozzarella balls or grapes with an edible googly eye
  • 'Mummy' hot dogs or sausage rolls wrapped in thin pastry strips
  • Clementines drawn with a marker to look like tiny pumpkins
  • A cauldron of green 'swamp punch' — lemonade with a splash of green juice and floating grapes

Easy Halloween decorations on a budget

You can turn a living room into a friendly haunted house with black bin-bag streamers, cotton-wool cobwebs stretched across corners, and a few paper-bag ghosts or printed bats on the walls. Pick just two or three focal points — the door, the games area, and the food table — and don't try to cover every surface. A little goes a long way when it's lit with a few warm battery tea-lights.

A 'spooky doorway' (black streamers hung across the entrance that each little monster has to push through) is the single best-value prop you can make — and it's the photo every parent keeps. Keep the lights up rather than down for this age; warm and glowy reads as magical, pitch-dark reads as frightening.

Party favours worth keeping

Skip the plastic tat that ends up in the bin by bedtime. A small treat bag with one or two useful bits beats a heap of throwaway trinkets every time — and it's usually cheaper, too.

  • A little bag of the sweets they 'won' from the games
  • A glow stick or a spider ring — cheap, and always a hit
  • A sheet of Halloween stickers or a mini pumpkin-carving activity
  • A personalised 'Monster Hunter' certificate with their name — kids adore seeing themselves named as the hero of the night

🛒 Your one-trip shopping list

Here's everything above, gathered into a single list so you can grab it in one shop or one online order. Much of it you may already own — the rest is a handful of cheap seasonal bits. This rough guide covers about eight little monsters:

  • Decorations: black bin bags for streamers, cotton wool for cobwebs, a few paper-bag ghosts or printed bats, and a pack of battery tea-lights
  • Food: pretzel rods and flaked almonds, mozzarella or grapes and edible eyes, sausage rolls and pastry, clementines, lemonade and a splash of green juice
  • Games: 6 toilet rolls and a small pumpkin for bowling, spare toilet-paper rolls for the mummy race, ring doughnuts and string, a box and a few 'monster guts' ingredients, a spooky playlist
  • Favours: eight treat bags, glow sticks or spider rings, a sheet of stickers each
  • The extras that make it: black and orange paper plates and napkins, and one printed 'Monster Hunter' certificate per child

The bit that ties it all together: one shared mission

The best kids' parties have one shared story that everyone plays through together — a reason for all the games, not just a list of them. A simple Halloween version: tell the little monster hunters that sneaky Count Snackula has hidden all the Halloween candy somewhere in the house, then hide clues that lead from one room to the next until they crack the final code and rescue the treats for the whole party to share.

You can absolutely build this yourself with hand-drawn clues and a cheap combination lock. It takes an evening of writing clues, hiding them, and testing that the trail flows — and it's genuinely satisfying when it clicks together on the night.

Common questions

What age is a kids Halloween party best for?

A themed Halloween party works from about age 4 up, but 6-9 year olds are the sweet spot: old enough to love a spooky story and solve puzzles, young enough that friendly monsters beat genuine scares. Keep everything spooky-not-scary for this age, dial the lights up rather than down for younger guests, and warn parents on the invite if you plan anything in the dark.

What games do you play at a kids Halloween party?

The reliable winners are pumpkin bowling with mini pumpkins and toilet-roll pins, a mummy-wrap race with toilet paper, doughnuts-on-a-string (no hands), a spooky sensory 'monster guts' feely-box, pin-the-nose-on-the-pumpkin, and a musical-monsters freeze dance. Mix two or three high-energy games with a couple of calmer ones so nobody melts down before the cake.

What are some cheap Halloween party ideas for kids?

Black bin bags cut into streamers, paper-bag ghosts and cotton-wool spider webs turn a room spooky for pennies. Toilet rolls become bowling pins and mummy-wrap ammo, a cardboard box becomes a monster feely-box, and renaming ordinary snacks ('witches' fingers', 'monster eyeballs') does all the theming for free. Most of a great kids Halloween party is things you already own plus a couple of pound-shop bits.

What's a good indoor Halloween party plan?

Halloween is made for indoors. Run pumpkin bowling down a hallway, the mummy-wrap race in the lounge, and a spooky treasure or clue hunt through a couple of rooms with the curtains drawn and a few battery tea-lights. Finish with one big shared mission across the house — a print-at-home Halloween escape room works perfectly because it needs no special kit, just a few rooms and a lock.

How do I keep a Halloween party fun but not too scary?

Lean into friendly monsters, silly ghosts and candy over gore and jump-scares. Keep lights on or use warm battery tea-lights rather than pitch dark, skip horror music for a playful 'spooky' playlist, and give every game a rescue-the-candy or help-the-friendly-monster framing. Tell nervous kids the plan in advance so nothing surprises them, and always have a bright, calm room they can step into.
