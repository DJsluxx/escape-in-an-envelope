Easter Party Games for Kids (Beyond the Basic Egg Hunt)

Every Easter party has the same arc: a ninety-second egg-hunt frenzy where the fastest kid scoops half the garden, one little one cries, and then everybody asks what's next. The fix isn't more eggs — it's a plan. Here's a complete do-it-yourself Easter party for ages 4-7: egg-hunt twists that keep it fair, five spring games with real instructions, snacks that barely need making, simple decorations, and one shopping list so you can grab the whole thing in a single trip.

🐰 An Easter party plan that actually flows

Ninety minutes to two hours is the sweet spot for ages 4-7 — this age runs out of battery fast, so front-load the movement and keep the ending calm. Open with a settling craft while everyone arrives, run the egg hunt early (it's what they came for), then games, then food, then one shared mission to finish.

A running order you can copy and adjust:

  1. 0:00–0:15 — Arrival: an egg-colouring or bunny-mask table while the group gathers
  2. 0:15–0:35 — The egg hunt (with a fairness twist — see below)
  3. 0:35–1:00 — Garden or living-room games: bunny-hop race, egg-and-spoon, bunny tail tag
  4. 1:00–1:20 — Spring picnic food and something sweet
  5. 1:20–1:45 — The main event: one shared Easter mission
  6. 1:45–2:00 — Prizes, baskets, and a calm wind-down before pick-up

Egg hunt twists that stop the grabbing

The classic free-for-all rewards the biggest, fastest kids and leaves the four-year-olds empty-handed. One rule change fixes it — pick whichever suits your group:

  • Colour-coded hunt — each child is assigned one egg colour and may only collect their own. Everyone ends with the same count, and you can hide the younger kids' colours lower and easier.
  • The golden egg — hide one special egg that's worth the big prize but takes clue-solving to find. The candy eggs are the warm-up; the golden egg is the story.
  • Count-limited hunt — 'everyone finds exactly five, then helps the others'. Turns the fastest kids into helpers instead of hoarders.
  • Clue-trail hunt — each found egg contains a slip of paper pointing to the next hiding spot, so the hunt becomes a trail the whole group follows together — the gentlest version for mixed ages, and the natural warm-up for the shared mission below.

Easter party games kids actually love

Beyond the hunt, you only need four or five games. These all work indoors or out, use cheap or already-owned materials, and land reliably with 4-7 year olds:

  • Egg-and-spoon race — the classic for a reason. Use hard-boiled or plastic eggs; younger kids get a tablespoon or steady the egg with a thumb. First across the line without a drop wins — drops go back three steps rather than out, so nobody's finished after two seconds.
  • Bunny-hop sack race — pillowcases, a start line, and a carrot-shaped finish flag. For little ones, ditch the sacks and just hop like bunnies — the wobble is the entertainment.
  • Bunny tail tag — every child tucks a 'tail' (a sock or ribbon) into their waistband. Tag by pulling tails: lose yours, do three star jumps to earn it back. Nobody sits out, so it runs itself for ages.
  • Carrot ring toss — stand three carrots upright in jars and toss rings (paper plates with the middles cut out) from a taped line. Points per carrot, littlest players stand closer.
  • Egg roll derby — everyone rolls a hard-boiled egg down a slope or a propped-up board; farthest roll without cracking wins. A hundred-year-old game that still gets shrieks.
  • Freeze-dance spring shower — spring playlist, pause button, and when the music stops everyone freezes in their best bunny pose. For this age, nobody's out — the poses are the point.

Spring snacks that are barely any work

Easter food names itself: a plain fruit platter becomes 'the bunny garden' with a printed label, and anything round becomes an egg. Keep it picnic-style — small hands, small portions, nothing that needs cutlery.

The thirty-second safety note for this age group: halve grapes and cherry tomatoes lengthways, skip whole nuts, and keep mini eggs for the over-fives — then relax and enjoy the party.

  • 'Bunny tails' — marshmallows on halved strawberries
  • 'Carrot patch' — hummus cups with carrot sticks planted upright
  • 'Nest cakes' — shredded-wheat and melted-chocolate nests with a candy egg on top (the one thing worth making, and kids can help)
  • 'Chick pops' — clementine segments with tiny paper beaks on picks (flags for the little ones)
  • A jug of 'spring rain' — cloudy lemonade with a few floating berries

Simple decorations and favours

Spring does most of the decorating for you. Concentrate on three spots: the front door (a paper 'bunny burrow this way' sign with paper paw prints leading in), the food table (a green paper-shred 'meadow' with the nest cakes on top), and the game area (crepe-paper streamers in two pastel colours). Skip anything inflatable — the paw-print trail gets more comments than any banner.

For favours, the hunt already did the work: each child leaves with their own egg haul in a paper bag they decorated at the arrival table. Add one packet of flower or cress seeds to plant at home — the favour that's still going a month later — and a named 'Junior Egg Hunter' certificate if you run the mission below.

🛒 Your one-trip shopping list

Everything above in one list, sized for about eight hunters:

  • The hunt: 40-50 plastic eggs in distinct colours (or 6 per child), fillers (stickers, mini eggs for over-fives, raisins for littles), one golden egg and a prize
  • Games: 4 spoons and spare eggs, 2 pillowcases, socks or ribbon for tails, 3 carrots and jars, paper plates for rings, a spring playlist
  • Food: strawberries, marshmallows, carrots and hummus, shredded wheat, chocolate, candy eggs, clementines, cloudy lemonade
  • Decor and favours: pastel crepe paper, green paper shred, paper bags and crayons for decorating, a seed packet per child
  • The extras: paper paw prints for the trail in, tent-card labels for the food, and one printed certificate per child

Making it work for ages 4 to 7

A four-year-old and a seven-year-old need different versions of the same party, and the trick is adjusting rules rather than running two events. For the younger end: hide their egg colour in plain sight at knee height, swap sack races for plain bunny hops, use hands-on-egg in the spoon race, and keep every game elimination-free so nobody sits out crying.

For the older end, add challenge instead of new games: their egg colour hides higher and sneakier, the golden egg needs a two-clue trail, throwing lines move back, and they can 'marshal' a station for the littles — six- and seven-year-olds take a deputised job very seriously, and it quietly solves your supervision problem too.

The bit that ties it all together: one shared mission

The best Easter parties end with one shared story instead of a fade-out. A simple version: the Easter Bunny has hidden the legendary golden egg, and the whole group follows a trail of clues from room to room — or bush to bush — solving one small puzzle at each stop until the final code opens the treat basket everyone shares.

You can build the trail yourself with hand-drawn clues and a bit of testing the night before: it takes an evening, and it's lovely when it works.

Common questions

What games do you play at an Easter party for kids?

The reliable set for ages 4-7: an egg hunt with a fairness twist (colour-coded eggs or a count limit), egg-and-spoon races, a bunny-hop sack race, bunny tail tag, carrot ring toss, and an egg roll derby. Mix two or three active games with a calmer craft or freeze-dance, and finish with one shared clue trail or printable Easter escape room so the party ends together rather than fizzling out.

How do you make an Easter egg hunt fair for different ages?

Assign each child one egg colour they alone may collect — then hide younger kids' colours low and obvious, and older kids' colours high and sneaky. Everyone finishes with the same haul and the four-year-olds aren't outrun. Alternatives that work: a 'find exactly five, then help others' rule, or a clue-trail hunt where the whole group follows one trail together. Keep a few spare filled eggs in your pocket for tearful emergencies.

What Easter games work indoors if it rains?

Almost all of them. Run the colour-coded egg hunt through two or three rooms, the egg-and-spoon race down a hallway, bunny tail tag in the biggest cleared room, and carrot ring toss anywhere with two metres of floor. The egg roll works down a propped-up ironing board. A printable Easter escape room is the strongest rainy-day finale because it needs no garden at all — just a few rooms, hidden clue cards, and a treat basket for the finish.

What do you put in Easter eggs besides candy?

Stickers, temporary tattoos, mini bouncy balls, hair clips, coins, tiny dinosaurs and folded jokes all fit a standard plastic egg — and for under-fives they're safer than mini eggs, which are a choking risk. The favourite non-candy filler is a clue: slips of paper that chain the eggs into a trail leading to one shared prize. For a party, mix roughly half candy and half fillers so the haul isn't pure sugar before lunch.

How long should an Easter party for young kids be?

Ninety minutes to two hours for ages 4-7. Shorter and the hunt-plus-lunch feels rushed; longer and the youngest guests melt down. Front-load the egg hunt in the first half hour — it's the thing they've been promised for weeks — then games, food, and one calm shared mission to close. A firm pick-up time on the invitation and a wind-down colouring table for the last fifteen minutes make the ending as smooth as the start.
