How to Make a Kids Escape Room at Home

A home escape room is one of the best-value kids' parties going — no venue, no pricey entertainer, just clues, puzzles and a code to crack. Here's exactly how to build one that actually works for your child's age, from a family that designs these for a living.

To make a kids escape room at home, build a clue trail: 5-6 small puzzles that each reveal one digit, chained so every solved clue points to the next. Read the digits in order to unlock a code-locked prize box. It takes about 30 minutes to set up with paper, a pen, and a few household items.

🔍 What a kids escape room actually is

Forget the idea of literally locking children in a room. A kids escape room at home is really a clue trail: a chain of small puzzles where solving one reveals where the next is hidden, until a final answer 'unlocks' the prize.

The mechanic that ties it all together is a code. Each puzzle gives up one number, and when the children collect those numbers in order they have the combination that opens the 'vault' — usually just a box, drawer or treasure chest with the party bags or cake inside. That one idea is what turns six separate activities into a single adventure with a real finish line.

You don't need an actual lock, though a cheap combination padlock adds a satisfying click. A printed keypad card the children 'press', or a box you open once they read the code aloud, works just as well.

🎨 Pick a theme kids will love

Theme first — everything else hangs off it. Choose something your child is already obsessed with; the puzzles underneath are identical, but a dinosaur maze beats a plain maze every single time. Easy, decorate-in-minutes favourites include:

  • 🦖 Dinosaurs — rescue the escaped baby T-Rex
  • 🚀 Space station — bring the lost puppy home
  • 🏴‍☠️ Pirates — crack the chest and find the gold
  • 🕵️ Spy or secret-agent — recover the stolen file
  • 🦄 Princess, mermaid or unicorn for a gentler, sparkly mission
  • 🎃 Seasonal — Halloween, Christmas or Easter

🎯 Match the puzzles to your child's age

The single biggest reason a home escape room flops is difficulty. Too hard and you get tears; too easy and it's over in five minutes. The trick is to tune every puzzle to the youngest player in the room and read clues aloud for anyone who can't yet read confidently.

As a rough guide:

  • Ages 4–5: picture-only puzzles, counting to five, matching and sorting, simple spot-the-difference. Keep the final code to three symbols and read everything aloud.
  • Ages 6–7: counting to ten, easy mazes, colour-by-number reveals, and a beginner picture cipher. A five- or six-digit code is fine.
  • Ages 8–9: multi-step ciphers, a bit of logic, trickier spot-the-difference and a full six-digit code. Let them work in teams with far less help.

🧩 Design a clue trail that ends in a code

Six puzzles is the sweet spot — enough to feel like a proper adventure, short enough to hold attention (aim for 30–45 minutes). Give each puzzle its own 'zone' in the house so the kids physically move around.

Hosting for four- and five-year-olds? Drop to three or four puzzles so the final code stays short and nobody loses steam — the one-puzzle-one-digit rule still holds, you just end up with a three- or four-digit code.

  1. Choose your mechanics — for example count, sort, maze, colour-reveal, picture cipher and spot-the-difference. Variety keeps it fresh.
  2. Make each puzzle produce exactly one digit — count the dinosaurs, there are four, so the first digit is 4. Puzzle two gives the second digit, and so on.
  3. Chain them so each solved clue names the next zone — that stops the trail being done out of order.
  4. Read the digits in order to get the final code.
  5. Lock the prize box (or a drawer, or a padlock) with that code, and put the party bags or cake inside.

🏠 Set up your space in 15 minutes

You don't need to redecorate. Print your zone signs, tape them up around the house, and ordinary rooms transform: a houseplant becomes 'the jungle', the kitchen 'the volcano', the bathroom 'the swamp'. Any home or garden works because the zones are portable.

  • Gather the basics first: a colour printer, paper, tape or Blu-Tack, and a box, drawer or chest for the prize (a combination padlock is optional).
  • Print clue cards in colour if any puzzle uses colour — colour-reveals and counting go wrong in greyscale.
  • Hide each clue at the zone named on the previous card.
  • Hand the kids only the first clue to get them started.
  • Keep a spare copy or two in case a card gets lost or a little hand gets it soggy.

⏱️ Run it smoothly on the day

Read the story opening aloud to set the mission — 'Rexy the baby T-Rex has escaped; find the cage code before he gets away!' Then give each child or team a code card to collect their digits as they go.

  • Set a friendly timer — aim to finish inside 45 minutes — but never let it run out on tears.
  • Have a gentle hint ready for every puzzle: a nudge in the right direction, not the answer.
  • For bigger groups, split into teams of two or three racing the same trail.
  • End on a high — the code opens the box, and everyone gets a certificate and a party bag.

⚠️ Common mistakes to avoid

Almost every wobbly home escape room trips over the same handful of things. Dodge these and you're most of the way there:

  • Puzzles pitched too hard for the age — the number-one party-killer.
  • Never solving the whole trail yourself first, so a clue turns out to be impossible on the day.
  • A trail that can be done out of order, so kids find clue five before clue two.
  • Relying on greyscale prints for colour puzzles.
  • No hint plan, so one stuck child stalls the entire party.
  • Making it too long — attention fades fast after about 45 minutes.

Common questions

What age can kids start doing escape rooms?

From about four with plenty of help — picture puzzles, counting, sorting and spot-the-difference, with an adult reading every clue aloud. By seven or eight, kids can work in teams and handle real ciphers with little help.

How long should a kids escape room last?

Plan the game for 30–45 minutes, plus roughly 15 to set up. Attention tends to fade past 45 minutes, so it's better to keep it snappy and end on a win than to pad it out.

How many puzzles do I need?

Six is the sweet spot for ages six and up — varied enough to feel like an adventure, short enough to hold attention. For fours and fives, three or four is plenty. Have each puzzle produce one digit of the final code so they add up to a satisfying finale.

Do I need to buy a real lock?

No. A printed keypad card the kids 'press', or a box you simply open once they read you the code, works perfectly. A cheap combination padlock is a nice extra if you want the click of a real unlock.

How many children can play at once?

Anywhere from three to eight is comfortable. For larger groups, split them into teams of two or three racing the same clue trail so no one is standing around waiting.

What if a child gets completely stuck?

Have a gentle hint ready for every puzzle and point them in the right direction rather than giving the answer, so they still get the thrill of solving it themselves. Pairing a younger child with an older buddy also helps.
