Christmas Party Games for Kids (Merry, Not Manic)

A kids' Christmas party in the middle of the busiest month of the year sounds like a lot — until you have a plan. You don't need elf-grade crafting skills or a grotto budget: you need five reliable games, food that names itself festive, and a running order that burns off the sugar before pick-up time. Here's a complete do-it-yourself Christmas party plan — a timeline that flows, games kids genuinely love, easy festive food, cheap decorations, favours worth keeping, and one shopping list to grab everything in a single trip.

🎄 A kids' Christmas party timeline that actually flows

December kids arrive pre-charged on excitement, so the structure matters more than at any other party of the year. Aim for about two hours for ages 5-8: open with a calm settling activity while everyone arrives, run the big-energy games early, feed them in the middle, and save one shared mission for the finale when they're primed for a story.

Here's a running order you can copy straight into your phone and adjust on the day:

  1. 0:00–0:15 — Arrival: a paper-snowflake or card-decorating table keeps early arrivals busy while stragglers turn up
  2. 0:15–0:50 — Active games while they're fresh: snowball toss, present-wrap relay, Santa says
  3. 0:50–1:10 — Festive feast and something sweet
  4. 1:10–1:40 — The main event: one shared Christmas mission across the house
  5. 1:40–2:00 — Prizes, treat bags, and a calm colouring wind-down before pick-up

Christmas party games kids actually love

Four or five games is plenty for two hours — over-planning just stresses the host. Mix a couple of runarounds with one or two calmer games so smaller guests can catch their breath. These are the ones that reliably land with 5-8 year olds, using things you mostly already own:

  • Snowball toss — scrunch up a big stack of white paper 'snowballs' and set out three buckets or boxes at different distances worth 1, 2 and 3 points. Kids take turns throwing five snowballs each; highest total wins. Instant resets, and the littlest players can stand closer.
  • Present-wrap relay — split into two teams, each with a cardboard box, a roll of cheap wrapping paper and tape. On 'go', each team races to wrap their 'present' neatly with a bow. Swap the box for a willing grown-up volunteer if you want the funniest photos of the season.
  • Santa says — Simon says, promoted to the North Pole: 'Santa says hop like a reindeer', 'Santa says stir the cocoa', 'check your list' (nobody move — Santa didn't say!). No materials, endless rounds, and you can play it while food is coming out.
  • Candy cane hook — each player holds a candy cane by the straight end in their mouth (or hand, for younger kids) and races to hook and lift other candy canes off a table edge. Most canes hooked in one minute wins — and doubles as a favour they keep.
  • Freeze-dance snowstorm — a Christmas playlist, pause button, and one rule: when the music stops, freeze like an icicle. Wobbliest icicle melts out — or for younger groups, nobody's out and everyone just holds their silliest pose.
  • Santa's sack — a pillowcase with 6-8 mystery objects inside (a bauble, a clementine, a toy car, a spoon). Kids feel through the fabric and whisper their guess; most correct guesses wins. A brilliant calm-down game after the relay.

Festive food that's barely any work

Christmas party food is a naming exercise, not a baking exam. Ordinary snacks become 'elf food' with a festive label and a cocktail-stick flag: strawberries become Santa hats, pretzels become sleigh runners, and a bowl of green grapes becomes 'Grinch fuel'. Print little tent cards for each dish and the naming does all the theming.

One thirty-second safety note so you can relax: for the youngest guests, halve grapes and cherry tomatoes lengthways, and swap cocktail sticks for paper flags.

  • 'Santa hats' — strawberries on a marshmallow base, dot of cream for the bobble
  • 'Reindeer noses' — a bowl of red grapes or chocolate-covered raisins with one shiny red sweet hidden in it (finder gets a cheer)
  • 'Snowman ladders' — banana slices stacked with a pretzel-stick 'broom' and chocolate-chip buttons
  • 'Elf wraps' — mini sausage rolls or cheese wraps with a green napkin 'belt'
  • A jug of 'melted snowman' — cloudy lemonade with a floating marshmallow or two

Cheap Christmas decorations that feel magical

You likely own the main decorations already — it's December. Rather than buying more, concentrate what you have into two or three focal points: the front door, the games area, and the food table. Warm fairy lights plus paper snowflakes taped to windows do more than any inflatable ever will, and a 'North Pole this way' paper sign on the hallway wall costs nothing but sets the whole story.

The single best-value prop: a 'workshop door' — strips of red and white crepe paper hung across a doorway that every little elf pushes through on arrival. It's the photo parents keep, and it turns walking into a room into entering the party.

Party favours worth keeping

Skip the plastic trinkets that don't survive the car ride home. One or two useful, keepable things beat a stuffed bag of filler — and usually cost less:

  • The candy cane they hooked in the game, plus a chocolate coin or two
  • A cheap Christmas cookie cutter with the recipe printed on a card tied to it
  • A sheet of Christmas stickers or a mini colouring book for the holidays
  • A personalised 'Official Elf' or helper certificate with their name on it — being named the hero beats any trinket

🛒 Your one-trip shopping list

Everything above, gathered into one list. Much of it is already in your cupboards in December; the rest is one supermarket run. This covers about eight little elves:

  • Games: a stack of scrap paper for snowballs, 3 boxes or buckets, 2 rolls of cheap wrapping paper + tape + bows, 10-12 candy canes, a pillowcase and 6-8 mystery objects, a Christmas playlist
  • Food: strawberries, marshmallows, red and green grapes, bananas, pretzel sticks, chocolate chips, mini sausage rolls, cloudy lemonade
  • Decorations: red and white crepe paper for the workshop door, paper for snowflakes, tape, and the fairy lights you already own
  • Favours: eight treat bags, chocolate coins, stickers, cookie cutters, and one printed certificate per child
  • The extras: red and green paper plates and napkins, and printed tent cards for the food table

Making it work for ages 5 to 8

Five-year-olds and eight-year-olds are different party animals, and most Christmas parties have both. The fix is to adjust the rules, not the games. For the younger end: shorten distances (snowball buckets closer), let them use hands instead of mouths in candy cane hook, drop eliminations so nobody sits out, and pair them with an older buddy in team games.

For the older end, add difficulty instead of new games: throwing lines move back, present-wrapping gets judged on neatness as well as speed, and Santa says speeds up until only one elf is left standing. One shared points chart with a prize for every player keeps the whole age range in the same party rather than in two separate ones.

The bit that ties it all together: one shared mission

The best kids' Christmas parties have one shared story everyone plays through together — a reason for all the games, not just a list of them. A simple version: tell your party of junior elves that the sleigh's launch code got scrambled and Christmas can't take off, then hide clues around the house that lead from room to room until they crack the final code and save the big night.

You can absolutely build this trail yourself with hand-written clues and a cheap combination lock: it takes an evening of writing, hiding and testing that the trail flows, and it's genuinely satisfying when it clicks on the day.

Common questions

What games can kids play at a Christmas party?

The reliable winners for ages 5-8 are a paper-snowball toss into buckets, a present-wrapping relay race, Santa says (Simon says with North Pole commands), candy cane hook, freeze-dance to Christmas songs, and a 'Santa's sack' mystery-object feely game. Mix two or three high-energy games with a couple of calm ones, and finish with one shared mission — a clue trail or a printable Christmas escape room — so the party ends on a story, not a sugar crash.

How do I plan a kids Christmas party on a budget?

Use what December already gives you: your existing decorations concentrated into two or three focal points, scrap paper for snowball games, and cardboard boxes plus one roll of cheap wrapping paper for the relay. Rename ordinary snacks with festive labels instead of baking anything ambitious, and make favours from the games themselves — the candy cane they hooked, a chocolate coin, and a printed certificate. Most of the party costs less than one shop-bought party kit.

What is a good Christmas party plan for a classroom?

Classroom parties run best at 60-90 minutes with stations: a craft table (paper snowflakes or cards), a games corner rotating snowball toss and Santa's sack, and a final whole-class mission. A printable Christmas escape room works especially well in class because it's team-based — split the room into elf squads of 3-4, give each squad the puzzle trail, and the first team to crack the sleigh code wins. No screens, no mess, and the certificates double as take-homes.

How long should a kids Christmas party be?

Two hours is the sweet spot for ages 5-8 — long enough for a settling activity, three or four games, food and one big finale, short enough to end before overtired meltdowns. December kids are already running on late nights and advent-calendar sugar, so resist stretching to three hours. If parents are staying, a firm pick-up time on the invitation ('2-4pm, elves collected promptly!') keeps the wind-down calm.

What can we do instead of a Christmas party gift exchange?

Gift exchanges get expensive and someone always cries over the swap. A shared mission beats it: hide 'Santa's lost presents' (wrapped empty boxes plus one real treat box) around the house and let the whole group hunt as a team, or run a Christmas escape room where cracking the final code unlocks one treat chest everybody shares. Everyone wins together, every child leaves with the same favour bag, and nobody compares presents.
